Florence Nightingale's Rose Diagram (1854-1856)

A quick description and history of this data visualization. This data visualization was developed by Florence Nightingale, who is renowned as the founder of modern nursing. Florence Nightingale's rose diagram was created during the 19th century when she was a nurse during the Crimean War. She was the first to notice the key role hygiene played in the spread of diseases that were causing mass casualties during the war.

The visualization itself can be broken down into various aspects, one of which showcases the seasonal effects on the mortality rate, with winter months having the highest number of casualties due to the cold weather preventing wounds and sicknesses from healing.

I chose to redesign this visualization because Florence Nightingale created it for a specific group of people: the king and queen, government officials, and members of the parliament, who weren't aware that the poor state of the hospital was causing the spread of disease that was killing thousands of men. It took me a lot of reading, watching videos, and staring at the diagram to understand it fully, so I believed I could redesign it for anyone to be able to look and understand what she was to convey with her visual.

This chart shows the causes of mortality during the Crimean War broken down by type (Disease, Wounds, Other). When clicking on the graph it will compare it to the total casualties recorded in that year.

This line chart shows the mortality rates per 1000 soldiers during the Crimean War. As you can see when you hover over the peak, After January 1855, the mortality rates drastically declined as the hospital started improving the sanitary conditions.

"Before Nightingale, disease destroyed military campaigns. Hospital barracks overflowed with waste. Linens returned from the laundry with vermin. Red tape choked the arrival of meager bandage and food supplies. Dying patients crowded on floors of blood-soaked straw. Dysentery, cholera, and typhus raged mortality rates to levels that exceeded London’s Great Plague." (Andrews 2019).
